How to Fix Choppy Video Calls: A 5-Step Guide

By Speedoodle Research · January 18, 2025 · 8 minute read

Few things are more frustrating than dealing with choppy, laggy, or frozen video calls during important meetings. Whether you're on Zoom, Google Meet, or Microsoft Teams, poor video quality can disrupt your work and damage your professional image. The good news is that most video call issues can be diagnosed and fixed with a systematic approach. This guide walks you through five essential steps to identify the root cause of choppy video calls and restore smooth, reliable communication.

Video quality problems stem from various sources: insufficient internet speed, network congestion, hardware limitations, or software misconfigurations. Rather than guessing, this methodical troubleshooting process helps you pinpoint the exact issue and apply the right solution. By following these steps, you'll not only fix your current problems but also gain the knowledge to prevent future disruptions.

Step 1: Test Your Internet Connection

Before anything else, you need a baseline. Use the Speedoodle tool on our homepage to check your speed, jitter, and packet loss. This comprehensive test measures the key metrics that affect video call quality: download speed, upload speed, ping (latency), and jitter (consistency of your connection). Unlike generic speed tests, Speedoodle is specifically designed to evaluate your readiness for real-time video communication.

Pay close attention to all four measurements. Download speed determines how clearly you see other participants, while upload speed controls how others see and hear you. For HD video calls, aim for at least 3-5 Mbps in both directions. Ping should be below 100ms for responsive conversations, and jitter should stay under 30ms to avoid audio-video sync issues. If any metric falls short, you've identified your first bottleneck.

Run the test multiple times throughout the day to identify patterns. Your connection might be fast in the morning but congested during peak evening hours when neighbors are streaming. Document these patterns—they'll be valuable when contacting your ISP or planning meeting schedules around network availability.

Step 2: Optimize Your Network Setup

Once you understand your baseline performance, it's time to optimize your network. The single most effective improvement is switching from Wi-Fi to a wired Ethernet connection. Wi-Fi is convenient but susceptible to interference from other devices, neighboring networks, and physical obstacles. An Ethernet cable provides stable, consistent bandwidth that eliminates many common video call issues.

If you must use Wi-Fi, position yourself close to the router and minimize obstacles. Switch to the 5GHz band if your router supports it—it's faster and less congested than 2.4GHz, though it has shorter range. Close doors to reduce interference and consider moving your router to a more central location in your home or office. Keep it elevated and away from metal objects or thick walls.

Check who else is using your network during calls. Background downloads, streaming services, online gaming, and cloud backups all compete for bandwidth. Ask family members or colleagues to pause heavy internet usage during critical meetings, or configure Quality of Service (QoS) settings on your router to prioritize video conferencing traffic.

Step 3: Update Your Hardware and Software

Outdated hardware and software are common culprits behind choppy video calls. Start by ensuring your router firmware is up to date. Manufacturers regularly release updates that improve performance and fix bugs. Check your router's admin panel or manufacturer website for the latest version. Similarly, update your video conferencing applications—Zoom, Teams, and Meet frequently improve codec efficiency and network handling.

Your computer's hardware plays a crucial role too. Video encoding and decoding require significant processing power. Close unnecessary applications and browser tabs to free up CPU and memory resources. Check your system's task manager during a call to identify resource-hungry processes. If your computer consistently maxes out its CPU during video calls, it might be time for an upgrade or to reduce video quality settings.

Don't forget your webcam and its drivers. External webcams often perform better than built-in ones, especially in low-light conditions. Ensure your camera drivers are current and properly configured in your video conferencing app's settings. Some apps offer hardware acceleration options that can significantly improve performance on supported systems.

Step 4: Adjust Video Call Settings

Sometimes the solution is as simple as adjusting your video conferencing settings. Most platforms allow you to control video resolution and frame rate. If your connection struggles with HD video, dropping to standard definition (480p or 720p) can dramatically improve stability. The trade-off in visual quality is often minimal compared to the improvement in smoothness and reliability.

Consider turning off your video entirely during periods of network stress. Audio-only calls use a fraction of the bandwidth required for video and are far more resilient to network issues. This is particularly useful during mobile or public Wi-Fi connections. Most platforms also offer options to disable HD video, limit incoming video quality, or reduce the number of simultaneous video feeds displayed—all of which can help conserve bandwidth.

Enable "computer audio only" mode and disable any unnecessary features like virtual backgrounds, which require additional processing power. Some platforms offer "low data mode" or "bandwidth saver" options specifically designed for challenging network conditions. Experiment with these settings to find the right balance between quality and performance for your specific situation.

Step 5: Consider Network Infrastructure Upgrades

If you've followed the previous steps and still experience choppy calls, it may be time to upgrade your network infrastructure. Contact your internet service provider to discuss faster plans or investigate whether fiber optic service is available in your area. Fiber connections offer superior speed, lower latency, and better consistency than cable or DSL.

For larger homes or offices, consider investing in a mesh Wi-Fi system. These systems use multiple access points to blanket your space with strong, consistent coverage, eliminating dead zones and handoff issues. Look for systems that support wired backhaul connections between nodes for maximum performance. Enterprise-grade access points offer even better reliability if budget allows.

If your router is more than a few years old, replacing it can yield significant improvements. Modern routers support newer Wi-Fi standards (Wi-Fi 6/6E), have more powerful processors, and include better Quality of Service features. For business use, consider a router with dedicated video conferencing optimization or the ability to create separate networks for work and personal devices.

Conclusion: Taking Control of Your Video Call Quality

Choppy video calls don't have to be a fact of life. By systematically testing your connection, optimizing your network setup, updating your tools, adjusting settings, and investing in appropriate upgrades, you can achieve the smooth, professional video calls you need. Remember to start with the Speedoodle speed test to establish your baseline and measure improvements as you implement each step.

Video call quality issues are almost always solvable with the right approach. Whether you're working from home, running a distributed team, or conducting client meetings, following this guide will help you identify and resolve the specific issues affecting your connection. Keep testing, keep optimizing, and don't hesitate to reach out to your ISP or IT support when you need additional help.
