How to Fix Choppy Video Calls: A 5-Step Guide

By Speedoodle Research · January 18, 2025 · 8 minute read

Is there anything more frustrating than a video call that constantly freezes, lags, or sounds robotic? When you're trying to lead a meeting or connect with family, choppy video quality can ruin the experience. The good news is that you can often fix it yourself. This 5-step guide will walk you through the most common causes of poor video calls and show you exactly how to solve them.

Step 1: Get Your Baseline - Test Your Connection

Before you start unplugging cables or blaming your computer, you need to know what you're working with. The problem is almost always related to your internet connection, but "bad internet" can mean many things. The three key culprits are:

  • Slow Speed: Not enough bandwidth to handle the video stream.
  • High Jitter: Inconsistent data flow, causing robotic audio and video stutter.
  • Packet Loss: Small bits of data getting lost on the way, causing momentary freezes.

To diagnose this, you need a specialized test.

Action: Go to our homepage, www.speedoodle.com, and run the free, one-click test. It takes less than 30 seconds. Write down your Upload/Download Speed, Jitter, and Packet Loss scores. This is your baseline.

Step 2: Ditch the Wi-Fi (If You Can)

Wi-Fi is convenient, but it's also less stable than a physical connection. Walls, distance from the router, and even your microwave can interfere with the signal.

Action: If your computer has an Ethernet port, find a network cable and plug it directly into your router. This is the single most effective way to get a more stable connection. Run the Speedoodle test again. If you see a significant improvement in your Jitter and Packet Loss scores, you've found your primary problem.

Step 3: Close Bandwidth-Hungry Applications

Your internet connection is like a highway. If too many cars are on it, a traffic jam is inevitable. Video calls are very demanding. Other applications running in the background might be consuming your bandwidth without you realizing it.

Common culprits include:

  • Streaming services (Netflix, YouTube)
  • Online games
  • Large file downloads or cloud syncing (Google Drive, Dropbox)

Action: Close all other browser tabs and applications that use the internet. Make sure no one else in your household is performing a heavy download. Then, test your connection one more time.

Step 4: The Golden Rule - Restart Your Router

It's a cliché for a reason: it often works. Your router is a small computer, and just like any computer, it can get bogged down over time. A simple restart clears its memory and can solve a host of mysterious connection problems.

Action: Unplug your router from the power outlet. Wait for 60 seconds (don't skip this part!). Plug it back in and wait for all the lights to turn back on and stabilize. This usually takes a few minutes. This simple act can often resolve high jitter and packet loss.

Step 5: When All Else Fails - Talk to Your ISP

If you've followed all the steps above and your Speedoodle test results are still poor (e.g., high packet loss or very low speeds), the problem may not be inside your home. It might be an issue with your Internet Service Provider (ISP).

Action: Call your ISP's support line. Don't just say "my internet is slow." Tell them: "I am experiencing [X]% packet loss and [Y]ms of jitter when running a WebRTC test, which is impacting my video calls." Using these specific terms shows them you've done your homework and helps them diagnose the problem on their end more effectively.

Your Path to Clearer Video Calls

Fixing choppy video calls is a process of elimination. By following these steps, you can systematically identify and solve the bottleneck in your connection. It all starts with knowing your numbers.
